Understanding Galactic H I 21 cm Emission

12 March 2025 · radio astronomy, HI 21cm

The 21 cm line of neutral hydrogen (H I) is one of the most important tools in radio astronomy. It allows us to trace the large-scale structure, rotation, and dynamics of the Milky Way — even through dense dust clouds that block visible light.

Why 21 cm?

When the electron in a hydrogen atom flips its spin relative to the proton, the atom emits a photon with a wavelength of 21 cm. This transition is extremely rare, but hydrogen is the most abundant element in the Universe — so the signal adds up.

My Drift-Scan Observations

Using the 1.5 m PICTOR radio telescope, I carried out 600 s drift-scan observations to detect the Galactic H I profile. The analysis included:

  • RFI removal
  • Gaussian profile fitting
  • LSR velocity correction
  • Column density calculation

My results matched the LAB Survey within ~15%, confirming the reliability of small-aperture drift scans for student-level H I studies.

Why This Matters

Understanding H I is essential for:

  • Mapping spiral arms
  • Measuring Galactic rotation
  • Tracing interstellar gas dynamics
  • Star-formation environments
