Transit Photometry: How We Detect Distant Worlds

10 March 2025 · exoplanets, transit photometry

Transit photometry remains the most successful method for detecting exoplanets, responsible for over 70% of all discoveries. It relies on a simple idea: if a planet passes between its star and our telescope, the star briefly becomes dimmer. Detecting this dip requires precise measurements of starlight — often to an accuracy below 1%.

How the Transit Method Works

A typical transit curve has three important features:

  • Depth: tells us the planet's size
  • Duration: tells us the orbital distance
  • Shape: gives hints about limb darkening and system geometry

My Experience with Ground-Based Photometry

During my work with ÇOMÜ Ulupınar Observatory, I performed transit observations using small-to-medium aperture telescopes. Surprisingly, even a 0.4–0.8 m telescope can detect the transit of a hot Jupiter with proper calibration, guiding, and exposure planning.

The Limitations

Transit photometry has challenges: weather, seeing variations, airmass, guiding drift, and instrumental noise can all mimic or hide signals. This is why differential photometry — using reference stars — is essential to remove atmospheric and instrumental trends.

Why This Technique Still Matters

Despite new methods like radial velocity and direct imaging, transit photometry remains the most accessible and scalable technique. It enables large-scale surveys, statistical demographics, and atmospheric studies through transmission spectroscopy.
