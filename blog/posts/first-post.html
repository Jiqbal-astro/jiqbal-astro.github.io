How Open Clusters Can Teach Us About Exoplanets

7 March 2025 · exoplanets, open clusters

Open clusters are among the most beautiful objects in the night sky — but they are also powerful astrophysical laboratories. During my graduate research, I explored the open clusters NGC 884 and NGC 869 in the constellation Perseus. These clusters, collectively known as the “Double Cluster,” offer a rare opportunity to study exoplanets in a young, coeval stellar population.

Why Observe Exoplanets in Open Clusters?

Most known exoplanets orbit field stars — stars that are not part of any cluster. While these discoveries are transformative, field stars come with uncertainties: they have different ages, metallicities, and stellar histories. Open clusters, by contrast, are:

  • Same age (often 10–100 Myr)
  • Same chemical composition
  • Same formation environment
  • Different masses and evolutionary stages

This makes them ideal for studying how planetary systems form and evolve in a controlled astrophysical setting.

My Work on NGC 884 & NGC 869

Using ground-based optical photometry from a 0.8 m telescope, I searched for short-period Jupiter-size planets (hot Jupiters). Although no exoplanet transit was found, the survey provided:

  • High-precision light curves for cluster stars
  • Detection of 25 variable stars
  • Identification of 4 new variable candidates
  • Constraints on the frequency of hot Jupiters in young clusters

Even a null detection is scientifically powerful — it suggests that hot Jupiters may be rare or dynamically unstable in very young environments.

What These Results Mean

Many exoplanet discoveries suggest planetary migration occurs early in a system’s history. If hot Jupiters are absent in populations younger than ~30 Myr, this helps refine our models of planetary formation and orbital dynamics. Clusters like NGC 884 and NGC 869 can therefore serve as important tests for:

  • Disc migration timescales
  • Planet–planet scattering
  • Stellar UV radiation effects
  • Mass loss from planetary atmospheres

This work taught me that exoplanet science is not only about finding new planets — it is about understanding the environments in which they form. Open clusters remain one of the most promising, yet underexplored, environments for advancing that understanding.
